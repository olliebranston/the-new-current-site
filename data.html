Carbon accounting

The UK’s hidden carbon footprint

The UK’s domestic emissions keep falling, but the picture looks less positive when we include the carbon embedded in what we consume. Per-person emissions are improving, but not by enough.

Territorial emissions

The UK’s domestic emissions are falling

On a territorial basis, the UK’s emissions have fallen significantly over time. This is the positive headline: less carbon is being produced within the UK economy.

Consumption emissions

The consumption story is less positive

When emissions are counted by what the UK consumes, rather than what it produces, progress looks much weaker. Some of the UK’s carbon footprint has effectively been shifted offshore through imports.

Per-person emissions

Per-person emissions are improving, but not enough

Adjusting for population growth makes the picture slightly better: consumption emissions per person have come down. But there is still work to do for the UK to become a truly net-zero economy.

Market signals

Clean power, complex bills

Wind and solar can undercut fossil generation because they do not need to buy fuel to produce power. That means cleaner periods often bring lower wholesale prices, although demand and system conditions still shape the final price.

Carbon intensity

Cleaner periods come and go through the day

Carbon intensity moves through the day as weather, demand, and system conditions change. Windy, sunny, and lower-demand periods usually mean cleaner electricity, while tighter periods tend to rely more on higher-carbon generation.

Wholesale price

More clean supply can mean cheaper power

When low-carbon supply is high, wholesale prices often fall because more zero-fuel-cost generation is available. But demand still matters: prices can stay high during busy periods or fall overnight even when carbon intensity is less favourable.

Generation mix

The mix explains the price signal

The generation stack shows what is driving the system at each point in the day. More wind, solar, nuclear, and interconnector output can reduce the need for fossil generation, helping explain both lower carbon intensity and lower prices.

However...

Lower wholesale prices are only part of the story. As the grid becomes cleaner, more intermittent, and more constrained, the cost of keeping the system reliable becomes more important.

Annual system mix

The grid is getting cleaner

GB electricity generation has shifted away from fossil fuels and towards renewables and other low-carbon sources. The long-run direction is clear: the power mix is greener than it used to be.

Consumer cost

But household bills have kept rising

On a fixed-consumption basis, the standard annual domestic electricity bill has moved higher. Cleaner generation has not translated neatly into cheaper bills for households.

Final takeaway

Cheap power is not the same as a cheap system

Renewables can reduce the cost of producing electricity when the weather is favourable. The next challenge is making sure the wider system costs of a cleaner, more variable grid are managed fairly and efficiently.

The transition is not just about replacing fossil fuel with clean generation. It is also about building the tools that keep a cleaner, more variable grid reliable: flexibility, storage, networks, balancing, and long-term investment support.

As electricity demand grows, these non-energy costs will matter more. The policy challenge is to reduce them over time through better grid planning, smarter flexibility markets, faster connections, and investment models that lower risk without overloading consumer bills.

In other words, the goal is not just cheaper clean power. It is a cleaner electricity system that is reliable, investable, and affordable for consumers.
