Benjamin Collie on Gas in Britain’s Power Transition

No disappearing act, but a new role to play

Sep 2025 · Power · Gas · Britain · Energy transition

In 2010, gas supplied nearly half of Britain’s power, around 175 TWh, while wind and solar together produced less than 10 TWh. Step ahead to 2024, and renewables delivered around 100 TWh, and gas generation had halved to about 90 TWh.

The catch is that we had more gas-fired capacity in 2024 than in 2010. Gas plants are running fewer hours on average, but remain essential for periods of sustained low renewable output.

Looking ahead, the UK’s 2030 “clean power” target will see unabated gas provide less than 5% of generation, though a similar amount of capacity to today is expected to stay online to safeguard security of supply.

For gas plant operators, success will depend on adaptation, through carbon capture, hydrogen, co-location with batteries or with demand, and new commercial models with a greater emphasis on monetising flexibility and grid services.

A Brief History of Gas in the Power System in Great Britain

For most of the 20th century, Britain’s electricity system was dominated by coal. While North Sea gas was discovered in the 1960s, its early role was largely confined to heating and industrial use. That changed in the 1990s with the so-called ‘dash for gas’, when liberalisation and falling gas prices made combined-cycle gas turbines (CCGTs) the preferred choice for new power stations. By 2010, 30GW of gas-fired capacity was supplying 175 TWh of Britain's annual power generation (nearly half the total), with coal supplying just over 100TWh and wind and solar about 10 TWh.

The EU Emissions Trading System (introduced in 2005) and the UK’s carbon price support (from 2013) reshaped the merit order. Because coal is about twice as carbon-intensive as gas, these policies disproportionately raised coal’s marginal cost and pushed gas towards a larger role. Wind and solar generation also benefited, scaling up rapidly.

By 2024, coal had been phased out of power generation, and wind and solar were supplying 100 TWh per year. Generation from gas had fallen to about 90 TWh, but gas capacity had increased to around 38 GW: the way gas plants were used had shifted, with each gigawatt of capacity running for fewer hours on average during the year.

This shift in usage reflects the rise of variable renewables: at times of high wind and solar output, it is not necessary or economical to run all the gas plants on the system. However, gas still underpins system stability, providing cover during multi-day or multi-week periods of low wind and solar output—periods current storage technologies like batteries cannot bridge cost-effectively.

Policy design underpins the way the power system has changed. Measures like Contracts for Difference (CfDs) give renewables price certainty and have enabled the expansion of wind and solar. The capacity market, launched in 2014, means power plants are paid for availability rather than just generation, and has enabled gas generators to remain economical even when they run fewer hours during the year, helping to ensure security of supply.

Today’s Gas Fleet

Britain’s gas capacity falls into three broad categories:

  • Combined-cycle gas turbines (CCGTs) – Most of the fleet, efficient for steady generation but less suited to frequent cycling.
  • Open-cycle gas turbines (OCGTs) – Simpler, cheaper, faster-starting units designed for short periods of peak demand.
  • Reciprocating engines (‘recips’) – Modular, flexible units, smaller in scale but quick to start, suited to very short ‘peaking’ runs.

Of ~38 GW of gas capacity, about 30 GW are CCGTs, with around 6 GW of peakers (OCGTs and recips) holding capacity market contracts.

Britain operates a marginal pricing system. Every half-hour, the most expensive unit needed to meet demand sets the price. When renewables dominate, wholesale prices collapse toward zero. When gas generation is marginal, power prices are driven by the cost of gas.

CCGTs now run fewer hours per year than they used to but at relatively high average prices. OCGTs and recips are designed to tolerate even lower utilisation, making money during scarcity events with even higher average prices.

For consumers, the results are mixed. While more renewables usually reduce average wholesale prices, consumer bills also reflect policy and system costs, like payments for CfDs, the capacity market, and balancing services. As the system shifts, a smaller share of bills goes to fuel, and a larger share reflects these policy and system costs.

Next steps for Gas Plants

In line with the UK's net zero ambitions, the government has set a 2030 target for "clean power", with an accelerated buildout of renewables and <5% of generation coming from unabated gas by the end of the decade.

Looking ahead, around 35 GW of unabated gas, about the same as in today’s fleet, is expected to remain on the system to safeguard security of supply. But by the 2030s these units will run at very low load factors, a sharp contrast with their historical role as mid-merit generators.

To keep unabated gas below 5% after 2030, there will be a need for lower-carbon sources of dispatchable power that can help meet demand during extended periods of low renewables output.

Companies that operate gas generation today are adopting a mix of strategies to respond.

  • CCUS (carbon capture, utilisation and storage) offers a credible but capital-intensive pathway for gas generators to provide low-carbon dispatchable power. UK policy support for CCUS is structured around industrial “clusters” with shared CO₂ transport and storage infrastructure. Location is critical: for example, plants like Keadby 3 (SSE and Equinor) and Connah’s Quay (Uniper) plan to take advantage of the East Coast and HyNet storage networks. Existing out-of-cluster plants face higher barriers, but some (like Uniper's Grain CCGT) are looking at the potential for retrofitting carbon capture.
  • Hydrogen provides another pathway to provide low-carbon dispatchable power. Plants like ESB's Carrington CCGT are looking at blending hydrogen into their fuel mix, while designs for 100% hydrogen turbines are progressing. Hydrogen is likely to remain more expensive than natural gas in the 2030s, but switching to run on hydrogen could be less capital-intensive than setting up CCUS: hydrogen may therefore be especially attractive as a decarbonisation route for OCGTs or recips that run only a few hundred hours per year. In the long term, hydrogen generation could act as a component of an inter-seasonal storage system, with surplus renewable electricity converted into hydrogen during the summer when solar power is abundant, and reconversion of hydrogen into electricity in the winter when heating demand is high.
  • Beyond directly providing low-carbon dispatchable power, there are other routes for today's gas plants to capture value in future. Many CCGTs sit on strong substations and high-voltage grid nodes. As their utilisation falls, their connections could become valuable platforms for other technologies. Some sites now pair CCGTs with batteries (e.g. TotalEnergies' West Burton B), while others are converting into synchronous condensers (e.g. Uniper's Killingholme). These shifts reflect the growing importance of grid services in a system with a rising portion of variable renewables.
  • Developments on the demand side also offer opportunities. Data centres, with their need for high-resilience connections, are increasingly co-located near legacy generation sites. Where policy allows, this could help drive investment in hybrid assets—CCGTs or OCGTs paired with batteries, CCS, or hydrogen.

What It Takes To Win

The shifting role of gas in power creates a mix of opportunities and risks for operators. Succeeding is likely to require market participants to excel in a number of key capabilities.

  • Operational agility – Running assets flexibly (cycling, rapid ramping, part-load efficiency) and adapting O&M practices to preserve reliability and efficiency despite low utilisation and high-cycling rates.
  • Government engagement – Shaping market design, securing regulatory approvals, and competing for government support in frameworks like the Dispatchable Power Agreement scheme for CCS.
  • Technology and capital project delivery – Repurposing existing sites with new technologies (e.g. CCUS retrofits, hydrogen conversion) and delivering new-build projects in a competitive, capital-intensive environment.
  • Commercial development – Building partnerships through the value chain, e.g. with data centres or hydrogen producers, to capture new value from existing assets.
  • Trading power, carbon and gas – Structuring revenue stacks across wholesale, capacity, balancing and ancillary services, maximising returns from fewer, high-value operating hours, and managing exposure to a global gas market that is in structural flux.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the single biggest barrier to the evolution of the GB power system today?

Development friction from planning and grid-connection delays. Important work to reduce this friction is happening now, for example through the connections reform process led by NESO.

What long-term policy change would help the system the most?

Support for development of hydrogen networks and large-scale storage so inter-seasonal balancing of power demand can be unlocked; this could also help gas peaking plants towards a credible decarbonisation path.

Which one metric should we keep an eye on as the industry evolves?

Ofgem publishes a breakdown of energy bills, showing how consumers' costs are split among wholesale costs, network costs, operating costs, policy costs and so on. The shift in the role of gas plants from providing day-to-day power supply towards providing backup reserves could be signalled by a shift in the balance of bills away from wholesale costs and towards policy costs, as gas plants will need to derive more of their revenue from the capacity market.
