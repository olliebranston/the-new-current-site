Piyush Patel on Europe’s role in global battery supply chains

Batteries and geopolitics: what Europe must do next

Sep 2025 · Batteries · Supply chains · Europe

The role of batteries in the energy transition

Increased renewable generation brings increased intermittency – the sun is not always shining, and the wind is not always blowing. Batteries’ bidirectional capability means they can soak up excess generation and add flexibility where grids are weakest.

Electric batteries also seem to have won the passenger-car contest on efficiency, cost trajectory and industrial momentum. Buses are electric at scale, heavy trucks are following with high-power charging corridors, and short-haul ferries look likely to lean on electric powertrains over alternatives.

Recent developments in vehicle-to-grid technology and commercialisation have brought these two opportunities together, as distributed energy resources become an increasing part of modern grids. These tailwinds create an increasing demand for efficient, cost effective and sustainable batteries.

Today’s supply chain landscape

China has established itself as a pioneering ‘electrostate’, dominating the battery space. Over two decades of co-ordinated policy have delivered roughly 85% of global cell capacity and a stronghold on graphite anodes. Many Chinese plants that were subsidised early are now benefitting from fully depreciated CAPEX and therefore can price near to (or in some cases at) marginal cost. Upstream, state-to-state deals have secured critical minerals across Africa and Southeast Asia, often outpacing new mines elsewhere that take time to permit and build.

Korea has been able to compete in the Nickel-Manganese-Cobalt (NMC) supply chains (while China is particularly strong in Lithium-Iron-Phosphate (LFP)). Historically this has been attractive, as Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) and consumers outside of China have preferred NMC for its higher energy density, but recently the lower-nickel and higher-cycling LFP has shown stronger growth. This effect is compounded by ESG concerns about the provenance of cobalt used in NMC.

The US has taken a more protectionist approach, prioritising industrial resilience first, decarbonisation second. Production tax credits and consumer rules favour domestic content and exclude ‘foreign entities of concern’. Its status as a ‘petrostate’ seems likely to be further preserved by the Trump administration. Consumer preferences are also somewhat unique; adoption lags Europe, as consumers prioritise range and performance over cost and sustainability, but the policy and investment environments continue to shift projects and talent in the West towards American soil.

Meanwhile, Europe is playing catch-up. It has world-class OEMs, relatively high EV adoption and the toughest sustainability rules, but few operating cell plants and thin upstream options. It sits somewhere between the electrostate and petrostate; rich in demand and regulation, but light on scaled manufacturing.

Europe’s supply chain options

Europe must act decisively and effectively to carve out its place in global battery supply chains. Tariffs and regulation alone will not close the gap. It has a significant option set in front of it and must find a balanced approach of “co-opetition” (to use Piyush’s term); a middle ground of competition and co-operation, to establish itself as a significant player in this dominated market.

Joint ventures on European terms

Europe struggles to close the cost gap with China powered by scale and fully paid-down assets. Regulation encourages local OEM production, but players should look beyond becoming a mere assembly hub. Joint ventures that bring Chinese process know-how into European-owned entities, grow local R&D, and protect recipes and data are key – China has a significant head start in the perpetual race for best IP, and JVs are one way to bridge it. This is becoming increasingly important as China tightens export controls on select next-gen technologies which it hopes to dominate markets with. Pairing these JVs with procurement that prices in embodied emissions can help Europe compete on a cost-per-kilowatt-hour and carbon-per-kilowatt-hour basis.

Friend-shoring the midstream

Growing midstream complexes in the Middle East or South Africa provide an opportunity to diversify away from reliance on China. Other auto hubs in countries with better labour and energy access and cost enable players to access materials more competitively.

Acquire distressed capability

China’s consistent undercutting of competitors has led to some businesses facing cash issues – overcapacity and squeezed utilisation is likely to open the door to the sale of capable assets. At the very least, buying technology packages and commissioning teams – then localising to build Europe’s first competitive LFP cathode plant etc. – could vault a committed player ahead of slower greenfield rivals.

Sodium – a genuine opportunity or a non-starter?

Sodium-ion cells have been floated as a technology that Europe could focus on and consolidate. It uses more abundant materials and tolerates wider temperature ranges, but its lower energy density combined with recent drops in lithium prices limits its attractiveness for passenger-car use. As evidenced by the recent difficulties faced by Natron Energy, Sodium may not be the silver bullet it seemed - whilst it may play a role in stationary storage and two-wheelers, lithium chemistries should remain the focus.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the best near-term commercial opportunity in the space?

In my mind there’s a strong small-scale opportunity, and a larger one. From the smaller perspective, circularity is interesting as Europe could look to aggregate lots of end-of-life cells, such as those from collision vehicles, production scrap etc. for commercial recycling opportunities. For bigger players, selective acquisition of distressed Chinese tech can accelerate growth.

What is the single biggest barrier?

Capital complexity: OEMs insist on multi-year qualification at production rates. Developers must finance gigawatt-scale pilots before offtake, then land offtake to finance full plants. Making a wrong bet in chemistry or customer can really harm your business case, and margins are slim in the face of major capex.

What policy change would help the industry the most?

Europe needs to commercialise the value of its domestic demand and market – and work with China to do this. Getting European and Chinese delegations to agree to a future model that works for both regions; encompassing a battery JV model within the context of broader trade deals and broader context of electrification. Europe as a potential customer base is attractive to Chinese players, and policy makers can look to leverage this to get out of our gridlock.

What is the most overhyped misconception today?

That investments in batteries are too risky because the rapid technology evolution makes things obsolete. Whilst the pace of technology evolution is rapid, when looking at the qualification requirements and standards for these materials and batteries to be used in vehicles, progress is slower. These things must be very safe, so qualification and homologation are unlikely to reduce from taking four to six years. These technical hurdles mean materials starting qualification today might not be in vehicles before 2030, at which point LFP and NMC will still dominate the lion’s share of the market.

Which one metric will you track monthly for the industry?

Following how Europe projects its capacity is interesting – it keeps growing from 700 GWh, to 1 TWh, to 1.2+ TWh – but people are double counting demand. A lot of people have very ambitious projections, but you have to see what’s actually being commissioned. When I look at assets I see significant proportions at medium-high risk, particularly when you consider unproven demand, challenging economics, execution complexity etc.. So, the ability to convert dollars into executing actual capacity has been challenged and is an interesting one to monitor.

For those interested, where can they learn more?

A lot of traditional trade press on this is very interesting; Benchmark minerals, Fastmarkets and the like do well. It’s also always good to get a flavour of what’s going on at the conference circuit. Or reach out to me!

Piyush Patel is a Director in EY-Parthenon’s Industrials & Energy Practise. Piyush has over thirteen years’ experience across the Power & Utilities, Mining & Metals, Automotive and Manufacturing and Battery sectors.
