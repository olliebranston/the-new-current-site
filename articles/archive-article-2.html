A New Age of Greenwashing in the Car Industry Thought Piece

Archive

How Sustainable are Electric Cars?

According to EU carbon dioxide regulations announced in April 2019, from 2030 European carmakers must have reduced average vehicle CO₂ emissions by 37.5%. This can only feasibly be achieved if car manufacturers begin mass production of electric vehicles (EVs) to offset the average. That year, sales of EVs surpassed 2.1 million units globally, and positive steps seemed to have been taken. However, criticisms by economists such as Hans-Werner Sinn and physicists like Crisoph Buchal, along with a history of greenwashing in the car industry and recent reports suggesting that hybrids might produce 2-4 times more emissions than their official values indicate that EVs might not be the clean, sustainable solution they appear.

Are electric cars really greener?

There are three main problems with electric cars from a sustainability perspective: manufacturing new cars, producing the electricity to power them and recycling parts once the cars are scrapped. This equates to a ‘lifetime’ carbon footprint which some would argue is worse than that of a petrol- or diesel-powered car.

Manufacturing

The problem with manufacturing is two-fold: basic production cost and mining resources. According to the Union of Concerned Scientists, electric cars generate more carbon emissions within the manufacturing process. They may even produce 68% more CO₂. However, since the energy used to build a car is far less than the energy used to run it over its life, after 6-24 months of driving the EV will have a lower lifetime usage of CO₂.

Mining

Mining is perhaps the most controversial issue of EV usage. EV batteries are made of rare earth elements (REEs) such as lithium and cobalt that depend on mining processes. Whilst from a pollution perspective EVs appear sustainable, unregulated mining practises highlight how there is a lot of work still to do in order to have a truly sustainable car industry.

Cobalt mining in the Congo has had problems with child labour and lithium has also had its fair share of ethical criticisms, being linked with increased desertification around the salt lakes of Latin America. Charities like Pact aim to work on increasing mining regulation, and the new German supply chain due diligence act is certainly a step in the right direction.

The Congo accounts for more than 60% of global cobalt production, almost a third of which comes from “informal” or “artisanal” workers. Artisanal small-scale mining (ASM) was legalised in 1981 by President Mobutu and arguably provides a vital livelihood for an estimated 42 million people around the world. However, it is grossly under-regulated and these artisanal workers are exploited by cobalt traders trying to capitalise on a demand that has tripled in the last decade. The flexible recruitment of artisanal workers by mining companies enables them to outsource labour costs and shift risks to miners, leaving them hugely vulnerable. Cobalt mined by these artisanal miners can also be sold to local traders, who in turn sell it to mining companies where it enters the global battery supply chain. Through these mechanisms miners as young as six, getting paid as little as 50p (or occasionally nothing at all) put themselves at risk daily. This can have tragic consequences, such as when 43 informal miners died in 2019 after a tunnel collapsed.

Electricity production

Whilst EVs often claim to be emission free, it is frequently forgotten where their power comes from. In the US 60.3% of the country’s energy comes from fossil fuels, and this proportion is even higher (71%) in the EU. If everyone drove EVs in the UK, using data from 2019, 57.1 billion kWh of energy would be required yearly, and hence for EVs to help bring about a green future this has to be more renewably sourced.

Recycling

E-waste is significantly more hazardous than general waste when not recycled and yet only 17.4% of e-waste is formally collected, let alone properly recycled. To address this, a number of positive steps have been taken. Tesla is continuing the construction of its ‘Gigafactory’ which it plans to make entirely powered by renewable energy sources and will contain its own battery recycling plant. It hopes that this can help to produce a closed-loop battery recycling process.

Volkswagen have similar goals, developing a new method to recycle the batteries that they estimate enables them to recover 95% of raw materials used.

The verdict

Despite these problems, overall EVs are still much greener than internal combustion engines (ICE) vehicles (in terms of pollution). Whilst some claim that these issues mean that over a life-cycle EVs are worse than ICE vehicles, the numbers behind their calculations are inaccurate. Although there is a huge amount of variation in potential calculations based on which cars are chosen and whose data you believe to be valid, best-case scenario and worst-case scenario computations show EVs to be much better.

The Future

It appears that now is a pivotal moment in the development of EVs: as they permeate more and more into mainstream consumption how can we maximise the benefit they can have on society and make them truly sustainable? Recent developments in hydrogen fuel cells are a positive start, but other areas need to be addressed. Deep sea mining has been suggested as an alternative source of REEs, allegedly providing a more ethical alternative to unregulated land mining. However, no one fully understands the risks. Anything in the path of the machines will be destroyed, whilst silt and sand churned up could potentially endanger marine life beyond the mining site, having catastrophic consequences for organisms feeding by filter. Greenpeace has warned of potential ‘irreversible environmental damage’, with even BMW and Volvo objecting to this technology. However, there are lots of positives to look forward to. Better recycling technologies are emerging and new policies from the EU and German government show how institutional agendas are improving. EVs are also much better than ICE vehicles in urban areas and with UN forecasts of 68% of the world’s population living in urban areas by 2050, EVs are becoming an increasingly attractive part of the future.

Conclusion

Therefore we can see how in terms of pollution, electric cars are clearly better. However, it is wrong to see the EV industry as green and sustainable as problems like the damaging production process and large amount of e-waste are still yet to be appropriately addressed. It is important that in the development of green, sustainable transport, procedures are established that are entirely sustainable, and that fashionable metrics such as CO₂ production are not focused on to the point of ignoring other parts of the process, such as mining damages. With this mindset we, as the consumer, can avoid being susceptible to greenwashing and help support a greener future.
