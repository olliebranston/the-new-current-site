Environmental Effect of Crypto

Archive

Bitcoin, Ethereum, Litecoin and other large cryptocurrencies use a proof of work consensus mechanism, which is the process used by multiple entities to reach an agreement about a fact. Cryptocurrency networks group multiple transactions into a block, which is built off the previous block (hence cryptocurrencies are blockchains). However, only one computer can produce a specific block of transactions and earn cryptocurrency. To establish which computer gets the block is the first to solve a complicated equation. The more powerful a computer, the more likely it is to solve the equation first and get the block (and reward). There are now over 1 million computers mining for bitcoin alone and this does use a lot of energy. But how unusual is this and should we be worried about it?

The anti-crypto narrative

An article published in Nature by Mora et al. claimed that ‘Bitcoin emissions alone could push global warming up by 2 °C in the next 30-60 years. Whilst nobody is actually sure how much energy bitcoin uses (since the entire process is decentralised and fairly accessible) it is estimated by the University of Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance (UCCAF) to be between 40 and 445 annualised terawatt hours (TWh), with a central estimate of about 130 TWh. This is more than the entire of the Netherlands, who use 121 TWh. This narrative has perpetrated itself into the general understanding of cryptocurrency, and has led to incidents such as Elon Musk stating that Tesla will no longer accept bitcoin as legal tender and the Sichuan region clearing out all the miners in the area.

Debunking Mora et al.

However, this Mora et al. paper has been massively criticised for making a number of incorrect assumptions. The data that was extrapolated for future predictions was largely based on the exponential adoption of bitcoin that happened at the peak of the 2017 bull run, meaning its popularity was unrealistically overestimated. The researchers also thought bitcoin would be used for almost one billion cashless transactions a day, all of which would use as much energy as an entire bitcoin block. In fact, you can fit around 3,000 transactions into a block (and hence they do not use the same amount of energy) and bitcoin can only process a few hundred thousand transactions per day meaning again its environmental effects were significantly overestimated.

Regarding the energy use, whilst pre-China clear out the energy consumption reported by UCCAF is probably accurate, this value is not that surprising (or worrying). At its peak bitcoin mining used a similar amount of energy to gold mining (which uses 131.9TWh). Idle devices in the USA use significantly more energy per year than bitcoin (200TWh) and when you consider the amalgamation of functions that cryptocurrencies really are, one might expect much more energy to be needed. Despite this, after the mass exodus from China, the electricity consumption has fallen to approximately 70TWh. In fact, cryptocurrency actually currently uses around half the energy that traditional banking uses (uses 140TWh per year). Thus we can see that the energy demands of cryptocurrency have been wrongly overinflated and there is less of an issue than one might be led to believe.

A synergistic opportunity?

According to Deutsche Bank Research, the Chinese National Energy Agency, Morgan Stanley and Coin Shares, 78% of Bitcoin’s electricity usage is from renewable energy resources (prior to the mass exodus from Sichuan). Compared with the 26% that is used generally from renewable resources, bitcoin seems to be relatively green. Crypto miners are also unique energy buyers, being able to work with a highly flexible and interruptible load, being completely location agnostic and paying in global cryptocurrency. Hence the Bitcoin Clean Energy Initiative suggest bitcoin mining could help accelerate the transition to renewable power, easing wind and solar deployment bottlenecks. Crypto mining could actually increase the amount of renewable energy produced, as increased bitcoin demand means that governments can justify setting up more renewable energy power stations, which will attract more miners creating a positive feedback loop that leads to a huge shift towards renewable energy generally.

China and the future

In June the Sichuan province in China, where 48% of the world’s crypto mining had previously taken place, banned bitcoin mining. Now China’s share of mining has fallen from 75.5% in September 2019 to 46% in April 2021, which is bad news because Sichuan had over a 90% renewables penetration in its energy supply, and it is predicted the majority of miners may head to Kazakhstan where 87% of their electricity is generated from fossil fuels. What might be better from a sustainability perspective is a shift to Texas, as it has lots of wind and solar farms (and hence also very cheap electricity). Renewable energy is actually now the cheapest energy, with the levelised cost of energy (LCOE) for solar and wind falling 90% and 71% respectively over the last decade. Unsubsidized costs of solar and wind energy are now 3-4 cents/kWh and 2-5 cents/kWh respectively, compared with coal or natural gas being 5-7 cents/kWh. Whilst this already demonstrates the economic incentives of ‘going green’, the mass exodus from Sichuan should be seen as an opportunity by global governments to attract miners and produce more renewable energy; it should be a priority of the US governments to tempt as many miners as possible away from Kazakhstan and to Texas, with the promise of cheap (and renewable) energy.

Conclusion

Hence, we can see how instead of providing us with a global warming dilemma, crypto mining gives us an opportunity to make a high demand, progressive industry entirely renewable and increase the world’s use of environmentally friendly energy supplies. We must no longer support the narrative of bitcoin being bad for the environment and should now try to capitalise on the opportunity afforded to us, making renewable energy sources much more prevalent whilst the economic incentive is present.
