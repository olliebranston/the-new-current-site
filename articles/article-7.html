Alwyn Hopkins on the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

CBAM explained: From carbon leakage to competitive strategy

Nov 2025 · CBAM · Carbon pricing · Trade · Sustainability

CBAM—the EU’s carbon border adjustment—extends EU’s carbon price to selected imports, aiming to avoid emissions offshoring. Reporting requirements are already in place, and costs phase-in from January 2026.

The geopolitical effects are already emerging: As exporters can credit a domestic carbon price, several jurisdictions are reforming ETSs to keep revenue at home, the UK plans its own CBAM from 2027, EU–UK ETS linkage is back on the table - while disputes are being raised at the WTO.

Counting carbon remains tricky: CBAM borrows installation-level accounting methods and excludes indirect emission sources for some commodities - dulling cost impacts for power-heavy metals.

“Carbon leakage” risk persists if firms import finished goods outside scope: Policymakers may extend coverage downstream or include indirect electricity to close that gap.

For EU importers, the priorities are clear: Understand exposure, secure authorisations and data, hedge ETS-linked cost risk, and work to decarbonise the supply chain and evidence lower embedded emissions.

The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) from first principles

Carbon pricing’s role in the energy transition

The energy transition needs market signals that reward low-carbon production and penalise high-carbon output. Carbon pricing does that by attaching a cost per tonne of greenhouse gas emitted. As such, it is often employed in hardest-to-abate industries, to stimulate decarbonisation. In Europe the flagship instrument is the Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), a ‘cap-and-trade’ system. Within cap-and-trade systems a limit, or ‘cap’ is set on greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions for companies within the jurisdiction. Companies are given (or must buy) allowances that they can ‘surrender’ to let them emit certain amounts of GHGs. Companies that pollute less than their allocation can sell, or ‘trade’, their spare allowances to companies that need more. Under the EU ETS, the allowance price – often in the €50-€80 per tonne range in recent years – turns carbon into a line item.

The problem: Carbon leakage

However, without compensatory policy measures, carbon pricing regimes run the risk of driving ‘carbon leakage’: Emitters move from jurisdictions with high carbon prices to areas with low/no carbon price to help keep their costs down. Production shifts, emissions don’t fall, and the jurisdiction with high carbon prices loses industry. Historically in the EU, Brussels has softened this risk with ‘free allocation’ of some allowances to energy-intensive, trade-exposed sectors. However, whilst this dulls the carbon leakage risk, it also reduces the effective carbon-linked cost for businesses, as they need to buy fewer allowances. This may lessen the decarbonising effect of the carbon price.

The solution: CBAM

CBAM provides a tariff-like adjustment on the embedded emissions of ‘in scope’ imports, in principle mirroring what an EU producer would have paid under the EU ETS. In this way CBAM bridges the cost gap between EU and non-EU production.

Specification and roll-out timelines

‘In scope’ is defined by customs classification: If you clear goods classified as “steel”, CBAM applies - but if you’ve used that steel to manufacture a vehicle classified for import purposes as a “car”, the CBAM does not apply.

Exporters can also credit a verified carbon price paid at home – so if a producer pays a carbon price in e.g. the UK, this should be taken off their due CBAM price.

The roll-out is staged. Since October 2023, importers have been in a transitional ‘report-only’ phase, filing quarterly declarations of volumes, origins and embedded emissions. From January 2026, the regime starts to cost – importers will need authorisation, begin accumulating a liability to surrender CBAM certificates priced off the EU ETS, and in 2027 will need to start surrendering those certificates against declared embedded emissions. Costs ramp as ETS free allocation falls in the early 2030s. Policymakers are also considering scope expansion around that time so that more downstream goods are captured.

Geopolitics and the global chain reaction

From a global response perspective, there are three major dynamics at play. Firstly, the regime incentivises governments outside the EU to implement / strengthen carbon pricing regimes domestically. As exporters can credit carbon prices at home, jurisdictions that do not have their own carbon pricing mechanism are missing the opportunity to claim the revenue for themselves. Secondly, governments are looking to integrate policy to simplify compliance. For example, the UK plans its own CBAM from 2027, and is exploring re-linking the UK Emissions Trading Scheme (UK ETS) with the EU ETS to simplify cross-Channel compliance. Finally, some nations have initiated disputes at the World Trade Organization (WTO) regarding CBAM’s relationship with the “Most Favoured Nation” rule. Nations with high emissions and high exports (such as India) will likely be hit hard by CBAM and have publicly voiced strong concerns about the mechanism. Regardless, the direction of travel seems to be toward a world in which embedded emissions carry an explicit cost in more jurisdictions, not fewer.

Counting carbon: product-level vs installation-level accounting

On paper, CBAM asks for the ‘embedded’ emissions per product – basing the price on how much carbon pollution was used to make one tonne of e.g. aluminium. In practice, the methodology inherits a lot from EU ETS compliance requirements, which measures emissions at a factory, or installation level – that is, the plant’s total pollution, not how much goes into each specific product. This retrofit in accounting methodology creates administrative challenge: Many suppliers already produce product carbon footprints or lifecycle assessments, however, the CBAM methodology is not aligned with many of these methodologies. For example, for several metals, indirect emissions from purchased electricity are currently excluded from CBAM’s methodology, despite electricity inputs dominating the carbon footprint (notably on aluminium). Not just does this create administrative challenge – but it also runs the risk of distorting the carbon price signal on these products.

Downstream and export carbon leakage: the scope stress test

Export carbon leakage could occur when manufacturers try to avoid CBAM by moving assembly outside of the EU and importing a finished good classified out of scope. This is a valid concern, with several policy levers to respond under discussion. One particular measure is to extend CBAM coverage downstream - so finished (or more heavily processed) goods carry a border carbon charge. However, in this instance, materiality differs by value-chain position. For raw materials, CBAM can swing margins. For high-value, advanced manufacturing, a modest carbon cost on metal inputs is diluted by value addition from engineering / assembly activities, software, and IP.

Next steps for EU importers

Following CBAM, EU imports have three primary areas of focus. First, compliance: secure 2026 authorisations, tighten customs commodity code governance and establish supplier data flows. Second, cost and risk: plan CBAM-allowance purchases against the ETS curve and hedge so volatility does not eat margin as free allocation falls, factoring in any indirect cost compensation. Third, decarbonisation, product, and manufacturing strategy: shift to lower-carbon inputs, pursue process upgrades where viable, and evidence intensity gains to customers.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the best near-term commercial opportunity in the space?

Enhance embedded-emissions performance on EU imports / exports to the EU. If you can beat the baseline, CBAM becomes a pricing and market access advantage.

What is the single biggest barrier?

Delayed and incomplete guidance – especially benchmarks and commodity-specific rules – which slows accurate exposure modelling and compliance planning.

What policy change would help the industry the most?

Publish the remaining legislation and benchmark tables, and set a clear position on future methodology and scope expansion – e.g. indirect emissions for metals / pathway for downstream scope.

What is the most overhyped misconception today?

That CBAM will rapidly re-wire advanced manufacturing / downstream supply chains. Near-term effects there will be limited, while raw materials feel the real bite in the short term.

Which one metric would you track monthly for the industry?

The ETS price (which anchors CBAM certificate cost) alongside verified embedded-emissions intensity by product line.

For those interested, where can they learn more?

The European Commission’s CBAM portal for legislation and guidance, supplemented by sector-specific technical notes from your industry association. Also, sign up to Alwyn’s webcast taking place on 2nd December!
