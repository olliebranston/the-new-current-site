Anthony Byrne on agentic AI for grid operators

Turning GB's grid data into governed, agent-driven workflows that cut cost, accelerate connections and strengthen resilience

2025 · AI · Grid operations · Data · Energy transition

Britain’s grid faces rising investment needs and growing operational complexity as generation decentralises and flexibility expands. Many network owners and operators have trialled narrow automations that do not change how decisions are made or how work flows.

There is an opportunity for radical overhaul: A data-centred operating model in which software agents, acting on a governed shared data layer, execute entire procedural tasks while people focus on stakeholder management, regulatory strategy and system risk.

The main constraints are poor data hygiene, internal resistance and heightened cyber exposure. With the correct approach, these can be overcome, and the productivity benefits of an agentic AI operating model can be realised.

The task at hand for network owners and operators

Britain’s network owners build, expand and maintain the transmission and distribution assets that move electricity from generation to consumption, whilst the system operator balances supply and demand in real time to keep the frequency stable and plays a longer-term role in planning the future grid. Both operate under five-year price controls set by the regulator, which define allowed revenues and encourage outperformance through incentives tied to reliability, connections and cost efficiency. Investment and operating cost is funded by grid participants paying charges on bills, with returns contingent on meeting licence obligations and beating benchmarks.

Decarbonisation is increasing the amount of intermittent generation and accordingly flexible demand. Operators must orchestrate more participants, manage greater uncertainty and accelerate connections while maintaining reliability.

Today, this is achieved by dedicated control rooms, asset management teams and capital delivery functions. Telemetry flows from field devices into operational technology, typically via SCADA (supervisory control and data acquisition) platforms. Asset data resides in enterprise systems, whilst work orders and plans can be siloed. Whilst there are AI pilots to automate parts of these processes, agentic AI can turn governed data into repeatable, documented procedures that improve cost, resilience and emissions.

The AI opportunity – agents run the procedures; people run the relationships

The energy transition, and particularly grid management within it, is very well placed to take advantage of the AI opportunity. Significant amounts of data must be processed at low cost to produce efficient management that aligns with overarching strategic goals. Significant amounts of time are currently spent on procedural tasks which allow human error to creep in.

Currently, AI is leveraged for fringe tasks – triaging emails, summarising documents, or answering queries – whilst leaving the underlying operating model unchanged. Data is distributed across different functions and systems, restricting the AI opportunity.

By building a single logical data layer, governed and permissioned, that unifies asset hierarchy, work queues, plans and live telemetry, the data architecture would then be in place for initial agentic roll-out. Software agents could carry out procedural jobs end-to-end within policy. They can generate standard designs, schedule and close maintenance, and update asset strategies when demand shifts. Humans can then focus on the conversations that matter – working with regulators, local authorities, customers and supply chains – whilst agents execute the routine work with traceable logic.

Obstacles that will decide the speed of value creation

Data

Data hygiene underpins the potential data architecture. At present, single sources of truth can be hard to come by. Project designs may not map to what is built, and as the automation cannot trust what it is looking at, people have to step in, slow things down, and add cost.

Better discipline in this area is required: Tightened project hand-back so crews return complete records, better use of sensors for data monitoring and the establishment of single sources of truth will allow for cleaner use of AI capabilities.

People resistance

Leaders may back AI, but managers who approve work and carry the risk understandably worry about losing oversight. This again results in extra checks, limited access for new tools, and delays.

Incremental roll-out, with an initial (insurance) duplication of effort, may be required to tackle this risk. Creating a digital twin of a function and running it alongside business-as-usual under identical policies allows for risk-free piloting. When the agentic route proves faster and at least as accurate, switching becomes a governance decision rather than a risk. The freed human capacity can then be leveraged in stakeholder work and exception handling.

Cyber

Moving actions from people to machine accounts increases the number of credentials, interfaces and dependencies that an attacker can probe. Poorly governed service identities, flat networks between enterprise IT and operational technology and opaque models can create pathways to misuse. This is of particular concern given the significance of the role of network owners and operators. Bad actors could create significant disruption by gaining access to these systems, and so the risk must be taken seriously.

Controls should be as strong as the automation; identity-centric access, strong segregation between OT and IT, and continuous adversarial testing are all likely to play a major role. Only when sufficient controls are in place should agent permissions be widened.

Next steps: A board-governed path to value

By overcoming these obstacles, operators can start to realise some of the productivity benefits available to them sooner rather than later. If players can put in place appropriate data architecture, they can select certain areas (e.g. planning) to begin piloting. By running the agents alongside business-as-usual, operators can build statistical confidence in proof that processes can be offloaded to these agents. Operators should then switch only after repeated outperformance and cybersecurity sign-off. Following the successful switch, widen permissions and expand the data model to adjacent processes so value compounds.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the best near-term commercial opportunity in the space?

The governed data core. Unifying asset, work and control information pays for itself by enabling agentic execution.

What is the single biggest barrier?

Data quality. Without trustworthy ‘as-built’ records and clean telemetry integration, credible automation is impossible.

What policy change would help the industry the most?

Price controls should recognise AI-enabled efficiency in both OpEx and CapEx and reward audited outcomes rather than preferred delivery methods.

What is the most overhyped misconception today?

That agentic AI is a distant prospect. With tight scope, proper governance and the right controls, it can deliver now. Early adopters will outperform in benchmarking, whilst laggards slide down the table.

Which one metric would you track monthly for the industry?

Revenue per employee, normalised for agent coverage, alongside connection times and reliability indices to reflect customer impact.
