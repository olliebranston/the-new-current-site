Meera Kotak on unlocking opportunities and addressing challenges for modular reactors

Modular reactors promise a productised buildout—if fuel supply, supply chains and licensing mature fast enough

Oct 2025 · Nuclear · Modular reactors · Energy transition

Modular reactors shift nuclear from one-off megaprojects to a product model: factory-built modules assembled on site for repeatable schedules, more predictable project costs, and steady output complementing wind, solar and storage. Demand and use cases for modular reactors span baseload capacity for cities; high-temperature heat for industrial processes; resilient power for remote sites and low-carbon power for data centres.

The challenge is going from first-of-a-kind technology to a true production line and customer order book, turning lessons learned into repeatable builds and sustainable growth, albeit predicated on fuel availability, supply chain mobilisation and licensing keeping pace.

Government selections and recent partnerships have been significant markers, building impetus as utilities and technology firms commit publicly. Maintaining momentum lies on the commercial programmes that will proceed – corporate PPAs, CfDs or RAB-style frameworks underwriting refurbishments and first deployments – and continued, targeted investment. Key enablers, such as cross-border licensing collaboration and strengthening the domestic HALEU and broader supply chain, will accelerate the pathway from demonstration to fleet deployment.

The nuclear transition: from one-off megaprojects to repeatable products

Small modular reactors (SMRs), micro-modular reactors (MMRs) and advanced modular reactors (AMRs) are set to change our relationship with nuclear infrastructure buildout as they will enable factories to produce modules that arrive largely complete. This can therefore reduce the risk of cost and timing overruns that have plagued recent large-scale nuclear projects, enabling pace on the pathway to securing the 24/7, low-carbon, high-capacity-factor power that will be needed in future clean power systems.

What this means for the energy transition

According to the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) typically produce up to 300 MWe per unit. They are generally based on proven light-water reactor technology, using standard uranium fuel and water as both coolant and moderator. Their design focus is on enhanced safety features, shorter construction times, and easier deployment—making them well-suited for grid-scale electricity generation, particularly in regions with smaller or isolated grids.

Micro Modular Reactors (MMRs) are significantly smaller, typically producing less than 20 MWe. These compact systems are designed for remote or off-grid applications, such as mining sites, defence facilities, or small communities, as they have long refuelling intervals (often 5–10 years), reducing the need for complex on-site operations.

Advanced Modular Reactors (AMRs) represent a broader category of next generation designs that depart from conventional water-cooled systems. They may use advanced coolants such as molten salt, liquid metal (e.g., sodium, lead), or high-temperature gas, enabling operation at higher thermal efficiencies and outlet temperatures. This makes AMRs attractive for non-electric applications, such as industrial heat, hydrogen production, or district heating, in addition to power generation.

These technological nuances mean each reactor type lends itself to distinct use cases and deployment contexts – from SMRs supporting regional grids, to MMRs powering isolated sites, to AMRs enabling future low-carbon industrial processes.

The current global playing field

Today, only a few modular projects operate, like China’s HTR-PM (entered operation in 2023) and Russia’s floating ‘Akademik Lomonosov’ (connected to the grid in late 2019, and supplying heat and power since 2020). These real-world deployments demonstrate that modular reactor concepts have moved beyond theory. However, the limited number of operational units also underscores the need for greater R&D, demonstration projects, and market engagement by new entrants.

Tech companies drive up demand

Big tech is helping drive demand – modular reactors’ ability to provide reliable, low-carbon load means they are well suited to power the expected spike from data centres. Microsoft’s 20-year nuclear PPA with Constellation to restart the Three Mile Island Unit 1 idle reactor illustrates how a creditworthy buyer can underwrite a refurbishment or new build; Amazon’s 17-year, up to 1.92GW deal with Talen Energy for power from their Susquehanna nuclear station in Pennsylvania points the same way, with explicit language to explore new nuclear. Google’s participation in a TVA (Tennessee Valley Authority) and Kairos Power deal for energy from Kairos’ planned Hermes-2 advanced reactor adds another marker. These deals don’t make SMRs bankable on their own, but they make the path clearer.

The intrinsic challenge

Transition technologies often face difficulties receiving first-of-a-kind (FOAK) financing – investors prefer technologically and economically proven technologies to back. Yet whilst financing projects can be challenging, each successful build accelerates the learning curve and brings the industry closer to standardised, cost-effective manufacturing. The sector is advancing rapidly, with significant R&D and valuable insights paving the way for Nth-of-a-kind (NOAK) deployments – where proven, repeatable production will unlock broad investor confidence and commercial viability.

Recent developments – such as the UK’s selection of Rolls-Royce as preferred bidder, Centrica’s joint development agreement (JDA) with X-energy targeting up to 6 GW of Xe-100 AMRs with Hartlepool as first site, and Holtec’s collaboration with EDF to explore SMRs at the former Cottam site – are strong signals of momentum and commitment. These initiatives represent real opportunities and mark important progress toward commercial fleets. As the industry continues to innovate and scale, these milestones bring us closer to realising the promise of reliable, low-carbon nuclear energy for the future.

The extrinsic pacesetters

There are three drivers that have a role to play in determining the tempo from first unit to fleet. First, fuel: many AMRs require high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU), which prior to the war in Ukraine was largely sourced from Russia. The US and UK have initiated domestic HALEU production at a small but growing scale: Centrus is now producing and delivering initial kilograms in Ohio and is contracted to continue at roughly a tonne per year, and Urenco is establishing the UK’s first domestic HALEU production facility with operations expected to begin by 2031.

Second, supply chain mobilisation: transitioning to a fleet model depends on rapid scaling and standardisation – requiring qualified suppliers, established fabrication capacity, and certified component production lines. A well-mobilised supply chain can shorten build times, reduce unit costs through learning effects, and de-risk delivery by creating repeatable processes across sites. Conversely, fragmented or immature supply chains will constrain manufacturing throughput, elongate licensing and quality assurance timelines, and erode investor confidence. In this way, supply chain readiness directly shapes how quickly the sector can move from proof-of-concept to commercially viable fleet deployment.

Finally, licensing: existing regulatory frameworks were built around bespoke, one-off gigawatt-scale plants and must evolve toward product-based approvals that, once approved, can be replicated with minimal reassessment. Both the UK’s Office for Nuclear Regulation (ONR) and the US Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) have recognised this need, recently strengthening cooperation to align methodologies, share safety evaluations, and accelerate cross-recognition of design assessments. Streamlined and harmonised licensing will be essential to compress timelines, lower FOAK risk, and unlock the economic promise of modular reactor fleets.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the best near-term commercial opportunity in the space?

Co-located industrial clusters and data centre campuses that value 24/7 power quality and can use high-grade heat.

What is the single biggest barrier?

It’s not necessarily the single biggest barrier, but a barrier we don’t talk about enough is licensing processes. Having these processes tailored to one-off gigawatt builds and not optimised for modular fleets may slow product-style deployment, especially when approvals need updating as the product design develops iteratively. The recent regulatory sharing arrangements have been positive steps forward, and hopefully the start of further developments.

What policy change would help the industry the most?

Mutual recognition and standardisation across markets. Fast-tracking demonstrably safe designs could unlock additional private investment, crucial for modular reactor development.

What is the most overhyped misconception today?

That SMRs aren’t safe – market participants are acutely aware of public sensitivities around nuclear energy, and modern reactor designs embed passive safety features and simplified systems to address these concerns. As recognisable utility and technology companies and brands sign long-term PPAs and JDAs, their rigorous due diligence has in turn become a form of reputational validation for the sector.

Which one metric would you track monthly for the industry?

Looking at the FOAK schedule and cost performance against baseline – essentially, how value is being captured per milestone on initial project delivery. Tracking progress across leading programmes in the US, UK and Canada provides a view of how timelines, costs and milestone achievements are evolving as the sector matures. Over time, observing learning-curve effects will be even more revealing – how efficiency and cost performance compound as projects progress from the second and third units through to FOAK and full fleet.

For those interested, where can they learn more?

Nuclear authorities and organisations have great resources – groups like the IAEA (International Atomic Energy Agency), NEA (Nuclear Energy Agency), NNSA (National Nuclear Security Administration) and WNA (World Nuclear Association) all have interesting resources. EY has also published thought leadership on nuclear revival. Or feel free to reach out!
