UN Gender Bond Thought Piece

Archive

On the 6th February 2020 UN Women published on their website the launch of “Women’s SDG Bond”, a new form of social bond which funded projects with social benefits dedicated to reducing gender equality. UN women have partnered with White & Chase, a global law firm, in order to achieve gender equality using ‘innovative financial tools’. The first generation of sovereign gender bonds have since emerged, but their impact and value can be debated – to what extent can a government bond produce meaningful change for women? Is this just another new form of greenwashing?

How good are sustainable bonds?

Sustainable bonds generally provide a great benefit for social or environmental projects and as such are becoming an increasingly attractive area to invest in. Responsible investing is becoming more mainstream, and these bonds provide investors with the ability to align asset allocations with sustainability objectives. However, there is a relatively broad criteria for what constitutes a green or social bond, and the reduced borrowing costs from ESG credentials mean that greenwashing is common within this industry. An understanding of the International Capital Market’s (ICMA) green and social bond principles (GBP and SBP respectively) enables us to properly evaluate these new sustainable bonds and should restrict greenwashing potential (but more on this later).

Should we care about sustainable bonds?

With sustainable bonds it can be difficult to see clear meaningful change as a product of investment; the money from the bond goes to back a macroscale project, the results of which are usually reported as general statistics from those who undertook the project in the first place. Seldom do we see testimonials from those who can directly link positive change back to investment in a sustainable bond. However, one would hope that this is more due to the complicated process of reducing inequality making it difficult to track the causes of positive change, rather than the absence of positive change. Sustainable bonds are becoming an increasingly attractive market, with more than $500bn worth of sustainable bonds issued in 2020 (including nearly 10 times more social bonds than were issued in 2019). The Italian government issued green bonds which were oversubscribed by 10 times and helped them raise a record €8.5bn. The real question is whether this increasing interest is due to sustainable bonds being an economically sound, socially responsible opportunity, or if it is because they are a socially acceptable form of finance on which governments can charge a ‘greenium’.

Greenwashing and loopholes in the sustainable bond industry

It is certain that some bonds issued look to exploit certain opportunities without sustainability as their primary concern. Due to some loopholes in the ICMA principles, the Islamic Development Bank’s (IsDB) framework criteria exposes itself to projects that can indirectly support fossil fuel elements (despite being aligned to these ICMA principles). With sovereign gender bonds, which were the main focus of the UN Women announcements, there is also a problem of accountability. Luxembourg and Egypt have both revealed plans to issue sovereign sustainable bonds. However, whilst Luxembourg’s Sustainability Bond Framework contains social bonds, it completely omits gender (with more focus on education, healthcare, and housing). Without unbiased reviews, as a sovereign issuer Luxembourg has proven susceptible to overlooking gender bonds. The Egyptian Ministry of Finance and National Council for Women are working with the UN Women Egypt Country Office to try to reduce financial gender equality, currently developing criteria that are expected to lean heavily on ICMA principles and projects for the gender bonds. These projects will be monitored and evaluated (to prevent loophole exploitation) but in a country where only 26% of women participate in the labour force (compared to 79% of men) and only 65% of women are literate, it seems there are more pressing issues of gender inequality than those that can be tweaked by social bonds – more drastic steps for change are required.

The role of microfinance

Chris McGarry is representing White & Chase in their dealings with the UN for these gender bonds and in an insight on the company website noted that microfinance CLOs (collateralised loan obligations) lend themselves naturally to reducing gender inequality. Women make up 73% of microfinance customers on average and so in the future we may well see these UN gender bonds head in this direction. Women are positively targeted by microfinance institutions (in a bid to reduce gender inequality in countries) and microfinance has proven successful in reducing poverty and hence gender inequality. It has been suggested by economists Zhang and Posso (2017) that an increase in the proportion of women accessing microfinance services by just 15% could reduce gender inequality in developing nations by half. However when considering averages one must not forget distributions and whilst microfinance goes a long way in reducing female poverty, for true gender equality women must be empowered to become wealthy (and not just all be sufficiently empowered to balance out an average).

Meaningful change in Mexico?

So how good can sustainable bonds, and specifically sovereign gender bonds, be? Mexico issued 21 green, social and sustainable bonds worth €750 million in September 2020 which are aligned with the GBP, SBP and Sustainable Bond guidelines. Whilst this framework is impressive for its social gap index made up of 11 sub-indicators (to look at disparity in different regions and target projects accordingly), €750 million split between 21 projects will have to be used incredibly efficiently to create change of any significance in a country with 55 million women living in it, especially when these women are four times less likely to be in employment, education, or training than Mexican men.

So what now?

This is not to say that sovereign gender bonds are pointless, or that all attempts to produce one have been failures. Sustainable economic opportunities are a massive step in the right direction and a number of this first generation of sovereign gender bonds have real positives, such as Mexico’s granular based framework or Egypt’s monitoring and regulation. By considering these attempts we can review the precedents set and hopefully produce better frameworks that create more meaningful changes for women around the world.
