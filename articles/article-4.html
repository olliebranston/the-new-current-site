Asif Sheikh on the case for CCUS

Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Storage: Economic barriers and policy playbooks

Oct 2025 · CCUS · Carbon markets · Policy · Energy transition

CCUS is moving from demonstration to deployment, but only where policy makes the maths work. The technology is largely proven, but economic hurdles restrict build-out.

Governments have taken different routes to viability. The US has tried per-tonne tax credits, Norway direct grants, and the UK a more prescriptive model that allocates risk. This ‘cluster’ model offers a potential solution – shared pipelines and stores spread fixed costs and reduce friction. However, off-cluster emitters will need links to the network, to avoid being left behind.

Setting up a bankable market is challenging. Market makers must set a clear, rising carbon price, share risks around, and steady revenues – all without driving industry overseas.

At the same time, capture plants need guaranteed buyers and pipelines and storage sites need minimum-use payments. Timelines need to align across the value chain or one delay stalls all. With predictable rules and support that fades as prices rise, private capital follows and projects can move from press release to construction.

CCUS and the energy transition

Carbon capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS) means capturing CO₂ emissions at source, transporting them (by pipeline, rail or ship), and then either storing them underground or finding productive uses. In decarbonising energy systems, dispatchable gas with CCUS is likely to play a key role in the short to medium term to ensure grid reliability. Blue hydrogen (hydrogen made from natural gas with CO₂ capture) may feature selectively in the longer term. Over time, however, the focus is likely to narrow to hardest-to-abate sectors; where process emissions are embedded and alternatives are scarce: cement, parts of steel, refining, and certain industrial heat applications, among others.

Technology ready, but economics lag behind

Much of the core CCUS technology is already proven but unlike technologies such as solar or wind, a steep “hockey stick” trajectory in efficiency gains is not likely. The constraint is cost.

  • Carbon prices in the UK this year have been between £31.54/tonne and £58.19/tonne (at time of writing).
  • By contrast, many real CCUS projects incur capture-transport-storage costs that are significantly higher, often well over £100/tonne. Precise numbers vary by site, emission type, scale, and geological storage distance, but this gap is substantial and hard to bridge.

As a result, government backed subsidies are needed to provide revenue guarantees. In the future, utilisation markets will provide pathway to commercialisation but it’s likely these will remain small and concentrated (e.g. aggregates and e-fuels).

In the absence of a business case, CCUS needs policy support

Since CO₂ storage itself doesn’t generate market revenue, and utilisation opportunities are modest, project economics often fail. Given that technological improvements are unlikely to fully close the cost gap soon, policy must bear much of the burden.

Governments around the world are experimenting with different subsidy/regulatory models:

  • United States: per-tonne tax credits (e.g. 45Q / related incentives), leaving much of the risk allocation to market players.
  • Norway: more direct grants, public support to get infrastructure in place.
  • UK: a regulated and prescriptive model, defining risk allocation across emitters, networks, and government, with contracts specifying who bears which risks.

All of these seek the same outcome, making CCUS viable without driving industries offshore.

Why clusters – and why isn’t everyone doing them?

The UK’s cluster model helps solve the physics of cost. Within the model, the government backs shared CO2 transport and storage networks and capture projects in designated industrial ‘clusters’ to attract private investment. Shared pipelines and shared geological stores spread heavy fixed costs across many emitters, whilst proximity reduces permitting friction and synchronises schedules. The UK’s initial focus on Teesside and the North West reflects that logic and the wider industrial-strategy goal: reinvigorate historic industrial heartlands with skills that already exist.

However, this model has trade-offs. Off-cluster emitters (e.g. cement works far from the cluster / transport network) face no affordable connection for years. Non-pipeline transport can ease the inequity by moving CO2 by rail or ship to coastal ‘beachheads’, but those supply chains need standards, hubs and bankable offtake too. Internationally, some countries may cluster naturally; others, with dispersed emissions or limited offshore geology, will rely on point-to-point links.

The market makers’ dilemma

Designing a bankable market is challenging. Market makers (regulators and finance ministries) could lift the carbon price signal so that not investing in becomes financially unattractive. In other words, the opportunity cost of not doing CCUS would be high enough to create its own business case. But they must do this without raising production costs so much that companies move operations abroad – an outcome known as carbon flight, or leakage. This is a fine balance and in a world where energy security is becoming paramount, not a decision that should be taken lightly.

They must also define a durable split of construction, performance and storage liability risks across capture plants, network operators and the state. And they must crowd in lenders by backstopping long dated revenue where carbon prices are volatile.

Cross-chain risk plays a significant role - a capture project can perform perfectly yet strand value if the pipeline slips or the store fails. Conversely, network investors cannot finance a pipeline against uncertain throughput. These risks don’t sit neatly with any single party, cannot be efficiently priced early on and compound across the chain. Until insurers and capital markets see operating data, governments will likely need to hold the rare, catastrophic and systemic tails.

What’s next for CCUS?

System level

At a system level, greater interconnectedness is likely. Not every country has the complete capability set – some have intense emissions but no geology for storage, others have a strong storage but thin industry. Harmonised carbon pricing, emissions-trading linkages and carbon-border measures will make cross-border CO2 movements easier to contract. That unlocks shipping routes from emitters to storage nations and expands potential ‘clusters’.

Project level

At a project level, the main blocker is revenue certainty. Capture projects need bankable offtake or support contracts that guarantee revenue for performance. Transport network operators need throughput floors or availability-based returns that reflect their regulated-asset character. All stakeholders require aligned construction schedules and contingent contracts that do not force expensive standstill in the event of slippage.

The technical capability exists. What remains is the policy architecture that shares risk credibly, raises the effective price of carbon predictably and does so without exporting strategic industries. Get that right and projects move from press releases to final investment decision (FID). Get it wrong and the pipeline stays virtual.

Quick-fire industry review

What is the single biggest barrier?

Long-term revenue certainty – bankable offtake for capture projects and predictable returns for networks.

What policy change would help the industry the most?

Co-ordinated, credible carbon-price trajectories paired with revenue support that declines as prices rise. But government must be careful to avoid carbon flight, particularly of strategically important industries.

What is the most overhyped misconception today?

That CCUS is simply a greenwashing lifeline for oil and gas companies. The cases for investment in this transitionary technology in process-emissions in cement, steel and other industrial processes are clear.

Which one metric will you track monthly for the industry?

Keeping a watchful eye on the project pipeline is critical. We’ve seen a record number of operational additions in 2025. As policy models evolve and private finance crowds in, we’re seeing projects become a reality.

For those interested, where can they learn more?

They can check out my policy point of view. The IEA’s CCUS reports, the Global CCS Institute’s status surveys and the Carbon Capture and Storage Association’s market updates are also all interesting.
